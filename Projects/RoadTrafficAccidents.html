Project Two: Road Traffic Accident Severity Classification

The Problem

Road traffic accidents are a serious global issue, contributing to high injury and fatality rates worldwide. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), "Every year, the lives of approximately 1.19 million people are cut short as a result of a road traffic crashs." While improvements in road safety measures and vehicle technology have helped reduce risks, understanding the key factors that contribute to accident severity is crucial for prevention and policy-making. By analyzing accident data, we can identify patterns and risk factors that could lead to safer roads and fewer fatalities.

Using the Road Traffic Accidents Dataset , this project aims to answer:

  • What factors most significantly influence the severity of traffic accidents, and how can they be used to predict accident outcomes?

This project aims to explore meaningful patterns in road traffic accidents and contribute to a deeper understanding of how various factors influence accident severity.

Dataset Information

The dataset contains 12316 rows, each representing instances of an accident, and 15 features. These features are important variables in determining the risk and severity of road traffic accidents. The features include:

Feature Explanation
Age_band_of_driver The age group of the driver involved in the accident.
Sex_of_driver The gender of the driver.
Educational_level The education level of the driver.
Vehicle_driver_relation The relationship between the driver and the vehicle.
Driving_experience The number of years the driver has been driving.
Lanes_or_Medians The type of lanes or medians present on the road where the accident occurred.
Types_of_Junction The type of road junction where the accident took place.
Road_surface_type The type of road surface at the accident location.
Light_conditions The lighting conditions at the time of the accident.
Weather_conditions The weather conditions at the time of the accident.
Type_of_collision The nature of the collision.
Vehicle_movement The movement of the vehicle at the time of the accident.
Pedestrian_movement The movement of pedestrians involved in the accident.
Cause_of_accident The reported cause of the accident.
Accident_severity The severity of the accident.

Data Preprocessing

The dataset I am using is a pre-cleaned version from Kaggle, which I selected after evaluating both the raw and cleaned datasets to ensure the preprocessing was consistent and effective. While inspecting the data using functions like .shape, .columns, and .head(), I noticed that the cleaned version removed columns with missing values (NaN or NA), a time-related column, and other less relevant features. This reduced the original 32 columns to 15, making the dataset more concise for analysis while preserving key information.

Modeling

I will be using a Random Forest model. Random Forest builds several decision trees and combines their results to improve accuracy and reduce overfitting. It handles imbalanced datasets well, is robust against missing data, and isn't heavily affected by outliers. However, it can be computationally intensive and harder to interpret. I chose both of these models because I thought they'd work best for my dataset, which is mostly categorical. Random Forest can best answer my question, "What factors contribute most to severe accidents?" because it gives me feature importance, helping pinpoint which factors matter most when it comes to severity.

Evaluation

To evaluate the performance of the Random Forest model, I used the following metrics: 1. Accuracy Score Accuracy measures the percentage of correct predictions out of all predictions made. It is a common evaluation metric for classification problems but may not be reliable in imbalanced datasets (as is the case here, with different levels of severity in accident data). 2. Classification Report The classification report provides precision, recall, and F1-score for each class (severity levels in the dataset). Precision measures the accuracy of positive predictions. Recall measures the ability of the model to find all relevant instances (how well it detects a specific class). F1-score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a balance between the two. These metrics are particularly useful in cases with imbalanced classes, like accident severity, where certain classes (e.g., severe accidents) might be underrepresented. 3. Confusion Matrix The confusion matrix helps visualize the true positives, true negatives, false positives, and false negatives. This allows us to understand how well the model performs across different severity levels and whether it tends to misclassify certain classes.

Impact Section

The impact of this project can be observed in several areas: Social Impact: By predicting accident severity more accurately, we can contribute to a better understanding of traffic accidents, leading to improved safety measures. If the model is deployed in real-world scenarios (e.g., insurance companies, traffic management systems), it could help predict the severity of accidents in real-time, allowing for quicker responses to severe accidents. Ethical Considerations: There is a risk that the model could perpetuate or exacerbate biases, particularly if the dataset underrepresents certain demographic groups (e.g., gender or age). For example, if young drivers are overrepresented in the dataset, the model might incorrectly associate younger drivers with higher accident severity, potentially leading to biased insurance premiums or unfair treatment in safety initiatives. Negative Impact: While the model aims to improve safety and risk prediction, it could inadvertently lead to unfair consequences if deployed without proper oversight. For example, the model's predictions might influence policy or decision-making in ways that disproportionately impact certain groups, such as young drivers, by increasing insurance rates or limiting access to safer driving environments.
